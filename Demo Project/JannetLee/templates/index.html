Introduction

Cyber-attacks have been quite prevalent in these past few years, and the increase in computing speeds has enabled cyber criminals to commit greater crimes in larger scales. Therefore, it is paramount for organizations with no specialized security team, to mitigate these risks, to be proactive in searching for hidden threats, and to react appropriately.

Statistically, an incident can cost an organization, on average, about USD 4,240,000 as of 2021 (Lohrmann, 2021). Furthermore, cyber-criminals are launching attacks worldwide on average, about 2,200 attacks daily (Norton, 2021). Therefore, small businesses need to protect their networks and assets as best they can to prevent or mitigate these attacks, as a successful attack on an organization has a great impact on their reputation and financial stability.

Therefore, we have developed this blogsite to provide SMEs the necessary information to patch security holes as they continue searching for professional advise.

What are these threats?

Malware

Malware includes multiple other threats, summon ones include threats such as virus, worms, trojans, keyloggers, rootkits, botnets, and logic bombs. These malwares are all different in how they behave.

For instance, a virus is a malware that can execute a certain malicious task when the file they are attached to is opened or executed, therefore, a virus requires a medium, such as a file, to spread.

A worm on the other hand, does not require medium to spread, instead, they will actively search for ways to infect other computers, worms can also carry payloads, which are the pieces of code that executes in a device (Veracode, n.d.).

Trojans are malicious programs disguised as trustworthy software, they work by presenting themselves as a program that is benign, however, when the victim opens the program, it could infect the victim's device.

Keyloggers are not usually malware, as they can be used as a monitoring software in an organization, however, a malware keylogger would log keyboard clicks and save it in a file, the file would then be sent to the attacker to be used to get account logins.

Rootkits are malwares that can plant itself deep in your device files, and it could provide the attacker root access to the infected device (Regan & Belcic, 2021).

Botnets are malicious softwares that infect devices, and use said device to utilize its resources to create DDoS attacks against networks. The botnets would first infect a device, and report to the botmaster, where he/she could then use the infected device, along with other infected devices, to launch attacks.

Logic bombs are malwares that infect a device and remains docile until a specific condition is met. These malwares can be triggered by multiple factors, such as date.

Ransomware

A ransomware works by first infecting a device, this can happen through malicious attachments in phishing emails (Checkpoint, n.d.). When a user opens the file, the ransomware will install itself on the victim’s device and encrypt all the files. After encrypting the files, the ransomware would then demand a set amount of money in order to decrypt the files.

An information to take note, is that it is not advisable to pay the demands for several reasons. Paying the ransom would not guarantee the recovery of your data, you would be paying criminals, and you would be a target for future ransomware attacks (NCSC, 2021)

Phishing

Phishing relies heavily on social engineering, therefore, it will typically involve less technical skills and more interpersonal skills. Attackers would typically first conduct some research on the victim’s information through several social platforms such as LinkedIn, and they would then email the victims (Gillis, 2020). The email would be based off of the information received through the reconnaissance phase in order to make it appear credible. The email would contain a link that would either lead the victim to a spoofed website created by the attacker, or a website that installs malware on the victim’s device.
